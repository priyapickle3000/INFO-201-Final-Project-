Introduction

Every year, millions of Americans die from causes that are both tragic and deeply patterned. According to the Centers for Disease control and prevention, the leading causes of death in the United States consistently includes cancer, disease, accidents, stroke, chronic liver respiratory diseases, Alzheimer’s disease, kidney disease, COVID-19, liver disease. While these categories appear as clean statistics, they represent deeply human experiences shaped by where people live, the resources they have, and the systems that surround them. Our project, goes beyond counting deaths, we are connecting national mortality data with state-level education and poverty indicators to explore how social and economic conditions relate to patters of mortality

We are working on a project that focuses on three important big-picture questions for Life and Death in America. What is the correlation between crude mortality rate per 100,000 and BA attainment by quartile? How does BA attainment by quartile affect crude mortality rate per 100,000 by ICD chapter? The second one being, what do shifts in leading causes of death reveal about public attention and community priorities? and the third one being, How does the poverty rate relate to mortality rates? BA attainment is calculated as the percentage of a population that has achieved a bachelor’s degree and is an indicator of the population’s overall educational attainment and socioeconomic status. Through the classification of communities into different education quartiles, disparities in mortality rates by educational attainment can be studied. ICD chapter refers to chapters in the International Classification of Diseases that classifies the underlying cause of death into broad disease categories including among others circulatory diseases, cancers and respiratory diseases. Our data sources for these questions include CDC (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention) datasets including The CDC WONDER Multiple Cause of Death database, which is a set of mortality data derived from U.S. death certificates. Information in the database can be obtained from the following website URL: https://wonder.cdc.gov/mcd.html. Along with this, we also used the CDC’s National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) national summary tables of leading causes of death. These are located on the following website URL: https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/fastats/leading-causes-of-death. The former dataset provides mortality information broken down by cause of death, time and demographics, while the latter summarizes the leading causes of death for U.S. residents. Our analysis delves into a question that has far reaching impacts on the health of a nation and its level of inequality. The US may be far ahead in the field of medicine, but it is often social factors and conditions that are more preventable that contribute to the leading causes of death. A person’s level of education, their economic status, and access to healthcare can all play a large role in dictating the extent to which he or she is exposed to risk factors and is able to receive medical attention when necessary. In our heathland analysis, we crunched the numbers to see what the relationships between education and mortality rates reveal and how social conditions can play a role in the health of a nation and its people. Like with any other type of data, mortality data also comes with its set of ethics and limitations. Deaths are recorded on death certificates, which can sometimes contain mistakes and inaccuracies concerning the underlying cause of death. Aggregate mortality data may also obscure important differences between populations that may exist when looking at smaller subsets, particularly for subpopulations that are underrepresented in a dataset. Also important to remember is that mortality data shows outcomes rather than telling us about the daily lived experiences of individuals in a population and the health detriments to which they are exposed. Therefore, it is crucial to bear in mind that every value in a mortality dataset is a life lost.

Datasets

Source: National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS), Centers for Disease Control and Prevention The CDC WONDER Multiple Cause of Death (MCD) dataset is derived from official death certificates stored in the National Death Index, which contains records from all 50 states and the District of Columbia. A death certificate lists a single underlying cause of death and, in many cases, one or more additional causes that are listed as contributing. The underlying cause is the condition most directly leading to death, whereas contributing causes represent other significant conditions present at the time of death. The underlying and contributing causes are assigned codes from the International Classification of Diseases, 10th Edition (ICD-10). This dataset contains information such the poverty levels and income of residence. The death records are collected from state vital statistics offices and are published by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) National Center for Health Statistics. The dataset now has over 3 million death records per year, which is the largest and one of the most widely used public health datasets in the U.S. We only use death records from recent years (about 2018-2023) in our analysis to study current trends in mortality. A strength of this data set is its national scope and rich details about the population of interest, which makes it possible to investigate mortality at the population level and at fine spatial resolution. A few potential sources of bias and missing data should be kept in mind. There may be some error in the recording of specific cause of death on the death certificate, and there are no data available on specific socioeconomic measures such as income or education at the individual level. This means that while many potential social determinants of health are included in the data set at the population or area level or derived from other sources, analyses are necessarily based on characteristics of populations or areas rather than of individuals.

Dataset 2: CDC Leading Causes of Death Summary Tables Public Access Link: https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/fastats/leading-causes-of-death.htm Created by: National Center for Health Statistics The CDC Leading Causes of Death summary tables are an annual national snapshot of death in the United States. Produced using final, edited and standardized data from the death records and population estimates, the tables show the leading causes, number, and age-adjusted mortality rates of deaths occurring annually within the United States. This dataset presents mortality data from millions of individual death records for each cause of death, aggregated into national trends on leading causes of death. The data generally covers the latest revised year for which mortality data is available and is based on deaths occurring among U.S. residents. Additional tables provide mortality data by subpopulation (age, sex, and race/ethnicity). The dataset presented in the paper provides a straightforward and tangible measure of the relative burden of different causes of death. The fact that the data are summarized in terms of rankings and percentages, obscures the extent to which the data can be dissected and analyzed to reveal further information that might exist within a region or community. As noted, the summary data is not sufficient to really provide for the analysis of the relationships between mortality rates and other factors, such as average years of education achieved. More detailed data would be required to properly address these types of questions. Additionally, when examining a country level, the data may not be granular enough to describe in full detail the conditions that give rise to the observed mortality rates.
